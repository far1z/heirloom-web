01

A dead man's switch, written into Bitcoin.

Your wallet is guarded by two keys and a clock. As long as you're active, only you can move the money. If you go quiet for months, the second key wakes up — and your heir can finally claim what's theirs. The rule lives on the blockchain, so no one has to be trusted to enforce it.

  1. Key 1

    You hold the owner key

    It lives only on your iPhone. You can send, receive, and spend your Bitcoin at any moment — exactly like a normal self-custody wallet.

  2. Key 2

    Your heir holds a waiting key

    Their key is powerless at first. It can only spend after a relative timelock — a stretch of your inactivity you choose: 3, 6, 9, 12, or ~15 months.

  3. Heartbeat

    You reset the clock

    A quick “heartbeat” transaction, on your schedule, refreshes the timelock. As long as you keep checking in, the heir key never activates.

  4. On-chain

    Bitcoin enforces it

    The whole arrangement is a Miniscript policy on the blockchain. The network — not a company, court, or server — decides when the heir may spend.

03

The trust model, stated plainly.

Non-custodial by construction

We never hold your keys or your coins. There's no balance for us to lose, freeze, or hand over.

Keys never leave your device

Both keys are generated and stored on-device. Nothing sensitive is transmitted to us — because there's no server that needs it.

Enforced on-chain

The timelock is a Bitcoin Miniscript policy. The network enforces the inheritance rules, not our infrastructure.

Works even if we disappear

The spending conditions live on the blockchain. Your heir can recover funds with the wallet descriptor alone — no Heirloom required.

Open source, so you don't have to take our word for it

Every line is public and MIT-licensed. Read it, build it, fork it. Trust the code, not the company.

Where we are today Full transparency

  • Signet only. Heirloom currently runs on Bitcoin's signet test network. Mainnet is not supported yet — please don't store real Bitcoin you can't afford to lose.
  • Not yet independently audited. The design uses standard, well-understood Bitcoin primitives, but it has not had a third-party security audit. Treat it as early software.
  • Verify before you trust. The whole point is that you don't need to trust us — so please read the code before relying on it.
